Name and Dates Publication School Quote Description
Aristotle, 384-322 BC Nicomachean Ethics, Book I, 1097a Ancient Greek philosophy "Every art and every inquiry, and similarly every action and pursuit, is thought to aim at some good; and for this reason the good has rightly been declared to be that at which all things aim." Aristotle believed that everything in nature possesses an intrinsic purpose or "telos." He argued that by understanding the natural order, we can discern the purpose of all things.
Thomas Aquinas, 1225-1274 Summa Theologica, Part I, Question 2, Article 3 Scholasticism "Whatever lacks intelligence cannot move towards an end, unless it be directed by some being endowed with knowledge and intelligence; as the arrow is shot to its mark by the archer. Therefore, some intelligent being exists by whom all natural things are directed to their end." Aquinas saw divine providence guiding all beings toward their ultimate ends, or "telos." He considered that the natural world, created by God, is inherently purposeful.
Jean-Paul Sartre, 1905-1980 Existentialism and Humanism Existentialism "Man is nothing else but what he makes of himself." Sartre argued that humans are responsible for defining their essence through their actions, emphasizing the importance of individual choice and personal responsibility.
Søren Kierkegaard, 1813-1855 The Sickness Unto Death Existentialism "The greatest hazard of all, losing one’s self, can occur very quietly in the world, as if it were nothing at all." Kierkegaard believed that living an authentic life required individuals to confront the anxiety of making choices, and that people must choose their own path rather than simply following societal norms.
Albert Camus, 1913-1960 The Myth of Sisyphus Existentialism "The only way to deal with an unfree world is to become so absolutely free that your very existence is an act of rebellion." Camus argued that life is inherently absurd, and humans must confront the absurdity of existence by finding meaning and purpose despite the lack of inherent meaning.
Simone de Beauvoir, 1908-1986 The Ethics of Ambiguity Existentialism "One's life has value so long as one attributes value to the life of others, by means of love, friendship, indignation and compassion." De Beauvoir asserted that individuals must create their own values and act responsibly to maintain a meaningful existence, emphasizing the importance of empathy and compassion in human relationships.
Maurice Merleau-Ponty, 1908-1961 Phenomenology of Perception Phenomenology "The body is our general medium for having a world." Merleau-Ponty believed that the "telos" of human beings is to better understand the world and our place in it through our embodied experience. He argued that our perception and understanding of the world are deeply rooted in our bodily experiences, and through reflection and interpretation, we can gain deeper insight into our existence.
Jean-François Lyotard, 1924-1998 The Postmodern Condition Postmodernism "Simplifying to the extreme, I define postmodern as incredulity toward metanarratives." Lyotard critiqued grand narratives, including those about "telos." He argued that individuals should be skeptical of any overarching narrative or theory that claims to explain the ultimate purpose of human life, and instead focus on local and specific contexts.
Alasdair MacIntyre, 1929-present After Virtue Virtue Ethics "The good life is the life spent seeking the good life." MacIntyre argued that the "telos" of human life is to cultivate virtues that enable individuals to achieve the good life. He emphasized the importance of moral character and the role of tradition in guiding ethical action.
Martha Nussbaum, 1947-present Women and Human Development Capabilities Approach "Capabilities are not only about the opportunities people have; they are also about what people are actually able to do and to be." Nussbaum proposed that the "telos" of human life is to achieve a set of capabilities that enable flourishing. She argued that societies should focus on providing the resources and opportunities necessary for individuals to lead meaningful, fulfilling lives.
Jürgen Habermas, 1929-present The Philosophical Discourse of Modernity Critical Theory "The task of a critique of modernity is to reconstruct the rational content of modernity that can be salvaged from the dialectic of enlightenment."
Richard Rorty, 1931-2007 Contingency, Irony, and Solidarity Pragmatism/Postmodernism "The world does not tell us what language games to play." Rorty was skeptical of any universal "telos" and argued that we should focus on creating a more just and humane society by engaging in dialogue and sharing our individual stories. He believed that by rejecting foundationalism and embracing the contingency of our beliefs, we can foster a greater sense of solidarity and empathy among people.
Immanuel Kant, 1724-1804 Groundwork for the Metaphysics of Morals, Preface German Idealism "Metaphysics must be preceded by a critique of pure practical reason, in order that we may know how far reason can extend itself in this domain, and what its limits are." Kant believed that humans have a moral duty to pursue their "telos," which is rational nature. He maintained that humans ought to seek the highest good and act according to moral principles.
John Dewey, 1859-1952 Democracy and Education, Chapter 12 Pragmatism "Progress is not the rule of the future as an abstraction; it is the possibilities of specific situations in their movement toward realization." Dewey focused on the continuous adaptation of human goals to changing circumstances as our "telos." He believed that individuals should strive for growth and development through practical experience and experimentation.
Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel, 1770-1831 Phenomenology of Spirit, Preface German Idealism "The True is the whole. But the whole is nothing other than the essence consummating itself through its development." Hegel believed that history was the progressive unfolding of Spirit toward its "telos," which is absolute self-awareness. He argued that the dialectical process of history brought humanity closer to realizing its ultimate purpose.
Ludwig Wittgenstein, 1889-1951 Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus, Proposition 7 Analytic Philosophy "Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent." Wittgenstein did not directly address telos in his work. His primary focus was on language and the limits of our understanding. However, one could argue that his philosophy suggests that humans should focus on clarifying our concepts and understanding the limits of language to avoid philosophical confusion.
Carl Jung, 1875-1961 Memories, Dreams, Reflections Analytical Psychology "Who looks outside, dreams; who looks inside, awakes." Jung proposed that the "telos" of human life is the process of individuation, which involves integrating conscious and unconscious elements of the psyche to achieve wholeness and self-realization. He believed that by facing our "shadow" and integrating it with our conscious self, we can attain psychological balance and fulfillment.
Alfred North Whitehead, 1861-1947 The Concept of Nature Process Philosophy "We think in generalities, but we live in detail." Whitehead proposed that reality is a process of becoming, and the "telos" of each entity is to actualize its potential through its connections with other entities. He viewed the universe as a dynamic, interconnected web of processes, with each actual occasion seeking its own self-realization.
Simone de Beauvoir, 1908-1986 The Second Sex Existentialism/Feminism "One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman." De Beauvoir believed that the "telos" of human beings is to create their own meaning and purpose through their choices and actions. She emphasized that individuals have the power to shape their own destinies and challenge societal norms.
Friedrich Nietzsche, 1844-1900 Thus Spoke Zarathustra, Part I, "On the Three Metamorphoses" Existentialism "I teach you the overman. Man is something that shall be overcome. What have you done to overcome him?" Nietzsche rejected the idea of a fixed "telos" and argued for personal self-realization. He emphasized that individuals should create their own meaning and purpose in life.
